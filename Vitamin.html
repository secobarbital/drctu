It is important to consult a physician before taking vitamins.

After the age of 50, seniors must learn a new alphabet. Whether it’s for purposes of antiaging, balancing one’s diet or in pursuit of overall better health, deciphering and properly ingesting the “vitamin alphabet” can be quite overwhelming for some.

Today’s fast-paced lifestyles often do not allow time to eat sensibly. Compounded by modern-day farming techniques and overprocessed foods, it is difficult to get complete nourishment solely from the diet. Taking a vitamin regimen is beneficial, helping to replace depleted nutrients required for good long-term health.

Following are the most common questions I answer for patients.

• “I take a vitamin C, a vitamin D, a little vitamin A. Am I taking the right ones?” I am concerned when I hear patients taking a little bit of this and a little bit of that. If you are unsure of what you should (or should not) be taking, ask a physician. Don’t guess. In most cases, it is safer to take a multivitamin formulated to work synergistically and effectively in the body.

The most definitive way to find out what the body needs is to undertake a series of blood tests. Over time, track blood work, exercise and food intake to determine what your body needs. An excellent starting point for this process can be found at www.anti-agingstraighttalk.com. Unfortunately, most of us do not have the time to track this information – and if that’s the case, then stick to a multivitamin complex. Generally, the body will use what it needs and discard what it doesn’t.

It is also important to understand the difference between fat-soluble vitamins and water-soluble vitamins. At high doses, fat-soluble vitamins such as vitamins A, D and E are toxic.

• “How effective is my vitamin, and what is an ORAC value?” Oxygen Radical Absorbance Capacity is the value given to a substance’s ability to oxidize free radicals. On a molecular level, free radicals attack healthy cells and are often the start of a downward spiral to illness and disease. With vitamins, the higher the ORAC value, the greater the antioxidant strength. Many studies suggest high antioxidant agents can help decrease risk of diabetes and cancer.

• “Why is there such a huge difference in prices for vitamins?” In general, when it comes to vitamins you pay for the purity, quality and bioavailability.

• “Why do I sometimes feel sick when I take my vitamins?” Some components of multivitamins may be more acidic, giving the sensation of heartburn. As a rule of thumb, take vitamins mid-meal with food and a tall glass of water.

• “Is it OK to take extra calcium?” Consult a physician before taking extra doses of any vitamin or mineral. Anything in excess can be detrimental to the body.

• “I take too many pills as is. How about those one-a-day vitamins?” One-a-day vitamins are simply too small to contain all the body requires for good nutritional health.

Take the time to read labels when selecting supplements, and chose quality over quantity. Be consistent – take vitamins and minerals daily, separate from other medications. Ask a physician if you need further direction.
